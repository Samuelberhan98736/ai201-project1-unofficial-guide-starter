CS MAJOR REQUIRED COURSE SEQUENCE (as of 2024 catalog)

Core Math Requirements (must complete before upper-division CS):
- MATH 2211: Calculus I — needed before most 3000+ CS courses
- MATH 2212: Calculus II
- MATH 2420: Discrete Mathematics (CS 3300) — critical for algorithms and theory
- MATH 2215: Statistics or CS 3315 — varies by concentration

Core CS Sequence:
1. CS 1301: Introduction to Computing (Python)
2. CS 1302: Software Development (Java)
3. CS 2340: Data Structures (Java)
4. CS 3410: Operating Systems (C)
5. CS 3300: Discrete Math for Computing
6. CS 4320: Algorithms — hardest required course in many students' opinion
7. CS 4850: Senior Seminar (capstone)

STUDENT REVIEWS OF REQUIRED COURSES

CS 1301/1302 — Introduction/Software Development:
These are the weed-out courses in name but not in difficulty. Most students do fine. Where people fail: not keeping up with programming assignments. The assignments build on each other. Missing two in a row puts you in a hole that's very hard to escape.

CS 2340 — Data Structures:
Where the real weeding-out happens. First time most students have to think algorithmically rather than just procedurally. Big-O notation, recursion, and linked structures are the hardest parts. Students who struggle here almost always have weak recursion skills — drill recursion problems before this class.

CS 3410 — Operating Systems:
The hardest required course for most students. C programming is unforgiving. Memory management errors don't crash the program immediately, they crash it later in mysterious ways. Debugging skills become critical here. Budget 15-20 hours/week during project phases. The payoff: you'll understand every CS concept better after this class. Students consistently say it was the most educational course in the major.

CS 3300 — Discrete Math:
Often taken alongside CS 2340. Heavy on proofs, logic, set theory, and graph theory. The material directly feeds into CS 4320 (Algorithms). Students who take it seriously here have a much easier time in Algorithms. The professor matters a lot for this course — check RMP before registering.

CS 4320 — Algorithms:
The capstone theory course. Covers dynamic programming, greedy algorithms, graph algorithms, and computational complexity (NP-completeness). Exams involve writing algorithm pseudocode and proving correctness/complexity. If you hate theory, this class will be miserable. If you like puzzles, you'll love it. Average GPA in this course is one of the lowest in the CS department.

CONCENTRATION TRACKS

Intelligent Systems (AI/ML focus):
Recommended electives: CS 4720 (Machine Learning), CS 4400 (Intro to AI), CS 4421 (Natural Language Processing), CS 4723 (Deep Learning if offered).
Career outcomes: ML engineering roles, data science, research positions.
Prerequisite warning: Linear Algebra (MATH 2400) is not required by the catalog but is practically essential for the ML courses. Take it as an elective.

Systems and Architecture:
Recommended electives: CS 4510 (Computer Networks), CS 4520 (Distributed Systems), CS 4490 (Advanced OS), CS 4330 (Compilers).
Career outcomes: Systems engineering, cloud infrastructure, embedded systems.
Hardest track academically. Most of the coursework involves C/C++ programming.

Software Systems:
Recommended electives: CS 4540 (Software Engineering), CS 4550 (Database Systems), CS 4560 (Web Development, if offered), CS 4800 (Mobile Development).
Career outcomes: Full-stack development, web engineering, SaaS companies.
Most directly applicable to industry jobs. Easiest track to get a first internship from.

COURSE REGISTRATION STRATEGY

High-demand courses that fill fast:
- CS 4720 (Machine Learning): Fills within 1 hour of registration opening. Register first thing.
- CS 3410 (OS): Multiple sections but all fill within the first day.
- CS 4320 (Algorithms): Limited sections, very competitive.

Backup strategy: If your preferred section fills, email the professor directly asking to be added to an override list. Some professors maintain these. Always ask — worst they say is no.

Tips for avoiding scheduling disasters:
- Don't schedule CS 3410 and CS 4320 in the same semester unless you enjoy suffering.
- CS 2340 and MATH 2420 in the same semester is common and manageable.
- CS 4850 (Senior Seminar) requires a project partner — coordinate with a friend before registering.
